Spotting misinformation

≈ 5 minArticle

False stories spread faster than true ones because they are engineered to. They aim at your emotions, not your reason. These five checks take under a minute and catch most of what the engineered stuff relies on.

1. Notice the surge

Misinformation is built to make you feel something immediately — outrage, fear, vindication. That surge is the tell. When a post makes your pulse jump, that is exactly the moment to slow down. Real news informs first; manufactured news inflames first.

2. Ask who is telling you

Scroll to the source before you believe the claim. Is there an author's name? A date? An outlet you have heard of — or a site dressed up to look like one? No source is a source. "People are saying" is nobody.

3. Read past the headline

Headlines are written to be shared, not to be accurate. Studies consistently find most articles shared online were never opened. The headline says what spreads; the third paragraph often says what actually happened.

4. Look for a second witness

Real events leave tracks everywhere. If something big were true, several unrelated outlets would carry it within hours. One obscure site with an exclusive nobody else has is not early — it is alone, and that means something.

5. Distrust convenient pictures

Most fake images are not doctored — they are real photos from a different time or place, re-captioned. A reverse image search (built into most browsers) shows where a picture first appeared. If a photo is doing all the convincing, check the photo.

Before you share

  • Did this make me feel a surge? Pause.
  • Who wrote it, and when?
  • Did I read past the headline?
  • Is anyone else reporting it?
  • Does the photo actually show what the caption claims?
