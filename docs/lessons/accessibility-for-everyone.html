Digital inclusion

Accessibility is for everyone

≈ 4 minArticle

Accessibility sounds like a specialist word — something for engineers, or for a small group of other people. It is simpler than that. Accessibility is the practice of building things so they do not lock anyone out. And here is the part most people miss: the features that help someone with a disability quietly help everyone else too.

Who accessibility is for

When we hear the word, we picture a permanent disability — blindness, deafness, limited movement. Those matter, and they are only part of the story. Disability is also temporary and situational. A broken arm makes a mouse hard to use. Bright sun washes out your screen. A loud bus drowns out a video. Aging eyes turn small text into a squint. Everyone moves through moments like these, so accessibility is not for other people — it is for you, on an ordinary day.

The curb-cut effect

There is a name for this pattern. When cities cut ramps into curbs for wheelchair users, something unexpected happened — everyone started using them: parents with strollers, workers with carts, travelers with suitcases, riders on bikes. A change made for a few ended up serving the many. That is the curb-cut effect, and it repeats all over technology. Captions rescue a video in a noisy room. Dark mode rests tired eyes at night. Voice input answers a message while your hands are covered in flour.

What the features are

The tools already exist, and most of them are free. A screen reader speaks a page aloud, so someone who cannot see the screen can still use it — which is exactly why a photo needs a written description; without one, the reader can only announce a meaningless file name instead of what is in the picture. Captions turn speech into text you can read. Text-size settings make words bigger — and on a well-built page, everything else reflows to stay readable. Voice control lets you tap, type, and scroll by talking. You will find all of them gathered in one place — the accessibility settings built into most modern smartphones.

Small habits that include

You do not need to be a developer to help. Most of this is about what you post and share. When a site offers an alt text box, use it — describe what is in the image in a plain sentence. Add captions to the videos you make; many apps will draft them for you. Keep important words as real text instead of baking them into a picture, so they can be read aloud, translated, and enlarged. And open your own phone's accessibility settings — something in there will make your day easier today.

Try it today

  • Add a short description the next time you post a photo.
  • Turn on captions for the next video you make or watch.
  • Open your phone's accessibility settings and switch on one thing that helps you.
