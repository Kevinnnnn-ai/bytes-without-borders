Data privacy

What cookies know about you

≈ 5 minArticle

A cookie is a small note a website leaves in your browser so it can recognize you the next time you visit. Most are harmless, some are genuinely helpful, and a few are built to follow you from one site to the next. This lesson sorts the useful cookies from the ones worth quieting — and shows you how to do the quieting without breaking anything you rely on.

The useful kind

When a site keeps you logged in, holds items in your cart between visits, or remembers which language you read in, that is a cookie doing honest work. These are first-party cookies — set by the site you are actually on, and read only by that same site. They store small preferences so you do not have to enter them again. Blocking every last cookie would break these everyday conveniences while protecting very little, which is why the goal is never zero cookies. It is the right cookies.

The following kind

Other cookies are set not by the site you are visiting but by a company whose code rides along on it — an advertising network, for example, that appears on thousands of sites at once. Because the same company is present everywhere, it can connect your separate visits into one thread. These are third-party cookies, and stitched together they build a picture: the shops you browse, the topics you read about, roughly where you are, and the hours you tend to be online. The familiar sign is the pair of shoes you looked at once, then saw in ads for a week. Nothing was hacked. A cookie simply reported where you had been.

What a profile is used for

That collected picture is called a profile, and its main purpose is ordinary: choosing ads you are more likely to click or buy. There is no need to picture anything sinister behind it. The arrangement is a plain trade — your attention is the product being sold, and your privacy is the price. Seeing the trade clearly is what lets you decide, site by site, whether a given one has earned it.

Quiet them without breaking the web

You do not have to choose between all cookies and none. When a cookie banner appears, look past the bright "Accept" button for the reject option. Sometimes it is one tap away on a second button. Often it is not — it hides behind a "Manage preferences" screen, or asks you to switch off dozens of toggles one at a time. That friction is deliberate and common, not a rare warning sign, so it is worth pushing through even when it takes more than a click.

Deeper down, your browser's privacy settings let you block third-party cookies outright, and many browsers now do this by default. First-party cookies keep working, so your logins and language settings survive untouched.

A private window — often called incognito — keeps cookies only until you close it, then forgets them. It is handy for a one-off visit or a shared computer. Be clear about its limits, though: it does not hide what you do from the sites you visit, and it does not hide you from your network, your workplace, or your internet provider. It forgets locally. It does not make you invisible.

Clearing your cookies now and then is healthy housekeeping. It will sign you out of sites, which can feel like something broke — it did not. Signing back in is simply the expected cost of a clean slate.

Try it today

  • Find the third-party cookie setting in your browser and see whether it is already switched on.
  • Reject cookies once on a site where you would normally tap accept.
  • Open one site you use often in a private window, and notice what changes.
