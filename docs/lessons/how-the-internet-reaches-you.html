Tech literacy

How the internet reaches you

≈ 5 minArticle

The internet can feel like air — always there, impossible to see. But it is not magic, and it is not weather. It is physical: cables, computers, and a stack of quiet agreements about how they talk. Picture the trip a webpage takes to reach you, and outages, slow days, and "why is this blocked here" all get less mysterious.

A request leaves your hands

When you tap a link, your device sends out a request — a short message asking a faraway computer to send back a page. That computer is a server: a machine whose job is to wait for requests and answer them.

Your request does not leap straight there. It hops from your phone or laptop to your router, out through your internet provider, and onto the wider network. The answer retraces the same trip back in pieces, usually under a second on a good connection.

Mostly cables, not satellites

When your message crosses a country or an ocean, it almost always travels through cable — thin strands of glass, called fiber, buried underground and laid along the sea floor.

Wireless is real, but it is usually only the last hop — your phone to a nearby tower, or your laptop to the router across the room. After that hop, the rest of the trip runs on cable. Satellites carry a small, growing share — vital where cable cannot reach, but the exception, not the everyday route.

Addresses and the phone book

Computers do not find each other by name. Every server has a numeric address — a string of numbers saying where it sits on the network. People are poor at remembering numbers, so we use names like example.com instead.

The system that connects the two is the Domain Name System, or DNS — the internet's phone book. When you type a name, your device asks DNS for the matching number before it can send its request. The answer is remembered for a while, so most repeat visits skip the lookup — but it is still one of the first things to check when a site will not load.

Why it slows or breaks

A trip with this many stops slows in many places. Distance is one — a server far away answers later than one nearby, because even light takes time to cross an ocean. Congestion is another: like a crowded road, a path carrying too much slows for everyone on it. A weak last hop — faint bars, a busy Wi-Fi network — can throttle an otherwise healthy trip.

This is why a site being "down" is worth a pause. The break might be the server, the path between, or your own connection — and which one it is decides who can fix it. If a single site fails while others load, the trouble is usually at that site's end — or a network or government is blocking just that address. If everything drops at once, it is likely close to home.

Why this matters

This explains something bigger than a slow page. People can only get online where the infrastructure reaches them — where cables run and towers stand. In much of the world it does not, or it arrives late and costs too much. The digital divide is not only about who can afford a device; it is partly a map of where the physical internet has, and has not, been built. Seeing the trip helps you see who gets left off the route — the gap Bytes Without Borders exists to close.

Try it today

  • Notice your own last hop: the bars on your phone or the Wi-Fi icon on your laptop mark the short wireless jump before the cables take over.
  • Next time something will not load, open a different site or another device — if only one thing is broken, the trouble is that site or the way to it.
  • Look up a map of the undersea cables just once. It quietly changes how the whole internet looks.
